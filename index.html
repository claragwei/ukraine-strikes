Ukraine
Crisis Map.

Russia's invasion of Ukraine has evolved from a territorial assault into a campaign affecting civilians and daily life. As strike patterns shift over time towards civilian infrastructure, understanding where and how violence impacts populations becomes critical as Ukraine is increasingly dependent on external support for recovery. Our Ukraine Crisis Map transforms publicly available conflict data into a spatial tool designed to support humanitarian analysis and strategic decision making in the ongoing war. It maps over 38,000 recorded Russian strikes across Ukraine from February 2022 to April 2026, integrating data from the Armed Conflict Location & Event Data Project (ACLED) and the International Organization for Migration (IOM). We layer incidents of strikes (sized by its fatality and colored by estimated target) and oblast level density and displacement counts in order to show how strikes have shifted. The sliding timeline allows humanitarian organizations, policy analysts, and researchers to visualize the spread of violence geographically. Beyond visualization, the platform provides a tool for humanitarian workers to create spatial strategies for response, identify priority evacuation corridors, high-need aid zones, and optimal locations for medical and refugee infrastructure. Our goal is not only to document the crisis, but to inform where resources, protection, and intervention are most urgently needed.

Solution Implementation

Our proposed solution for this crisis utilizes this visualization to establish a plan that proposes actionable humanitarian responses. First, we generated potential medical hubs sited at the intersections of displacement flows such as highways and in places that are geographically accessible: high IDP cities such as Lviv, Kyiv, and Dnipro, as well as conflict adjacent areas like Kharkiv and Zaporizhzhia. Second, shortest path evacuation corridors based on starting location and target location. Although we are limited on actual route information, our objective is to create a system that can give basic directional assistance to get to target sites. We generated the medical hubs on routes that avoid high density strike clusters and connect major roads. Lastly, we labeled known border crossings and zones where aid is most likely necessary due to proximity to frontline regions. Our main goal was to test how AI can be used to quickly create fast but effective tools with data alone that can help humanitarian aid and coordination efforts.
